Prologue

Once upon a Delhi morning…

She arrived in 1989, in Delhi — a city of noise and warmth and improbable colour — though her family's heart was always tucked away in the green folds of Kangra, Himachal Pradesh. She grew up in Laxmi Bai Nagar, on streets that knew her name, and somehow, from the very beginning, she carried something the world couldn't quite hold down: an enthusiasm for life so stubborn and bright it bordered on rebellion.

What follows is not a complete story — no story ever is. It is a scrapbook. A few pressed flowers from the long, wonderful walk that has been her life so far.

Before the love story

The solo years

Long before the rings and the vows, there was just her — a girl with dreadlocks one summer, schoolbook ribbons another, a Harley under her, and the whole world still ahead of her.

She was already, even then, the most interesting person in any room. The kind of girl who tried every version of herself — the school-uniform girl, the dreadlocks girl, the houseboat-in-Kerala girl, the rides-the-arcade-Harley girl — and somehow, beautifully, all of them turned out to be true at the same time. These are the pages from before. The chapters she wrote on her own.

A note in the margin

And of all the people in all her years, there was one she loved most of all: her Dadu. The man who held her in photographs and in memory long after photographs end.

A chapter all his own

Bhai

Every good story has a partner-in-crime. Hers came built-in.

There are some people you don't choose — they arrive with you, and somehow turn out to be exactly who you would have picked anyway. Her brother is that. Co-conspirator in plush-toy raids, fellow tea-picker in the hills of Munnar, late-night Delhi bike companion, the one whose face is pressed against hers in mehndi-stained photographs. The kind of bond that doesn't need explaining — only photographs, and a thousand inside jokes you'll never quite hear the punchline to.

The whole picture

Her family

The people who taught her how to laugh — and never quite stopped.

Look closely at any happy person and you'll find a family somewhere behind them, holding the camera. Hers is loud in the best way — the kind that turns balcony selfies into choreography, Holi into a yellow-powdered war, and a European train ride into the world's quietest party. Mum, Papa, brother and her: four people, infinite group chats, one running joke that has lasted decades.

And the wider tribe

Cousins & Chachu

Because in Indian families, "family" is a very generous word — and thank god for that.

Chapter Three

A craft, fourteen years in the making

What she does for a living — though "living" undersells it considerably.

Sakshi is a design leader. That's the short version. The long version is that for over a decade she has been quietly shaping how brands look, feel and behave in the digital world — leading designers, writers and strategists, and finding the precise point where creativity meets business sense and neither has to apologise to the other.

She works at the intersection of branding, user experience, creative strategy, and — most recently — generative AI. She believes design should be beautiful and useful, that great teams are built on trust, and that the best ideas usually arrive sideways.
